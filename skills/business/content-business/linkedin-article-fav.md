# LinkedIn Article

## What This Skill Does

Writes LinkedIn long-form articles (800-1,200 words) in Ragnar Pitla's signature thought leadership style. Ragnar's articles are read by enterprise decision-makers, AI practitioners, and ERP professionals. The style is confident practitioner, not commentator. Built on real implementations, not hot takes.

## The LinkedIn Article Format

### The Hook (First 3 Lines)

LinkedIn shows approximately 3 lines before the "see more" fold. Those 3 lines determine whether anyone reads the rest.

**Weak hook:** "AI is transforming enterprise ERP. Many companies are adopting Copilot Studio and D365 to improve their operations."

**Strong hooks:**
- Start with a bold, specific claim: "80% of enterprise AI projects fail for the same reason. It's not the technology."
- Start with a counter-intuitive insight: "After 12 Copilot Studio deployments, I've learned that the hardest part isn't the AI. It's the business rules."
- Start with a precise real-world observation: "Last week, a Fortune 500 finance team showed me their invoice processing workflow. 3 FTEs. 500 invoices per week. Manual matching. In 2026."

The hook must make the reader think: "Wait, I need to know more about this."

### Article Structure (800-1,200 words)

```
Hook (3 lines, before "see more" fold)
  ↓
Context: Why this matters now (100-150 words)
  ↓
The core framework or insight (400-600 words)
  ↓
Real example from actual work (150-200 words)
  ↓
The "so what": what to do next (100-150 words)
  ↓
Call to action (1-2 sentences)
```

## Content Categories

### Category 1: AI Strategy / Agentic Enterprise
Topics: agent-first thinking, automation vs agents, agentic enterprise, AI maturity model

**Voice notes:** Frame as paradigm shifts, not incremental improvements. Challenge conventional wisdom. Build a framework readers can use themselves.

**Example angle:** "The 3 questions I ask before recommending an AI agent vs a Power Automate flow"

### Category 2: Agentic ERP
Topics: D365 AI agents, invoice matching agents, period-close agents, procurement agents

**Voice notes:** Practitioner depth. Specific process names (Source to Pay, Record to Report). Real before/after metrics from deployments.

**Example angle:** "What happens when you put an AI agent in front of your D365 AP process"

### Category 3: Copilot Studio Deep-Dives
Topics: parent/child agents, description-based routing, Niyam pattern, Dataverse integration

**Voice notes:** Technical but accessible. Architecture diagrams described in text. Code-adjacent without being code-heavy.

**Example angle:** "The Niyam pattern: why I stopped hardcoding business rules in agent instructions"

### Category 4: Enterprise AI Adoption
Topics: AI transformation roadmap, getting buy-in, measuring ROI, failure patterns

**Voice notes:** Leadership perspective. Lessons from real deployments. What actually works vs what sounds good in theory.

**Example angle:** "The 5 signs your enterprise AI program is theater, not transformation"

## Writing Rules (Ragnar's Voice)

1. **Confident practitioner tone.** "I've built this. Here's what I learned." Not "some companies are finding that..."
2. **Framework thinking.** Structure ideas as frameworks (3 questions, 5 phases, 2x2 matrix). This makes content memorable and shareable.
3. **Specific over general.** "AP clerk spends 6 hours matching invoices weekly" beats "manual processes are inefficient."
4. **Never use em dashes.** Use commas or periods to separate clauses.
5. **One idea per article.** Don't try to cover everything about AI agents in one post.
6. **End with action.** Readers should know exactly what to do after reading.
7. **Disclaimer on Microsoft content.** If the article references Microsoft work, add: "Views expressed are my own and do not represent Microsoft's official position."

## Checklist: Before Publishing

- [ ] Hook in first 3 lines creates curiosity or challenge
- [ ] Article has one clear central idea (not 5 disconnected points)
- [ ] At least one specific real example (not hypothetical)
- [ ] Framework or structure makes the content memorable
- [ ] No em dashes anywhere in the text
- [ ] Confident practitioner voice, not hedged commentary
- [ ] Clear call to action at the end
- [ ] Microsoft disclaimer added if referencing Microsoft work
- [ ] Length between 800-1,200 words
- [ ] Tags/hashtags added (3-5 relevant ones)

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Article doesn't get engagement despite good content | Weak hook, reader never gets past "see more" | Rewrite the first 2 sentences. Test: would you stop scrolling for this? If not, rewrite. |
| Article reads as generic AI commentary | No real examples, no practitioner specifics | Add a "from my work" section with specific numbers, a real project scenario, or a lesson that surprised you |
| Article is too long and loses readers mid-way | Trying to cover too much | Cut to one central idea. Move secondary points to a follow-up article or comment thread. |
| Voice sounds formal and corporate | Editing out the personality | Read it aloud. If it doesn't sound like how you'd explain it to a colleague, rewrite that sentence. |

# LinkedIn Article — Example

## Scenario: Why Enterprise AI Projects Fail (and What the Niyam Pattern Fixes)

**Who:** Ragnar Pitla, writing for his LinkedIn audience of enterprise architects, D365 practitioners, and AI decision-makers
**Where:** LinkedIn article format; targeting 1,200-1,800 words; audience reads on mobile during commutes
**The Problem:** Ragnar has been watching the same enterprise AI failure pattern play out across customers. He wants to write the definitive LinkedIn article on why it happens and position the Niyam pattern as the structural fix — not a sales pitch, but a practitioner's honest post-mortem. He has the ideas in his head but needs the full article drafted in his voice.

**Prerequisites:** None — this is a content creation task.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Write a LinkedIn article in my voice about why most enterprise AI projects fail and what the Niyam pattern fixes. My voice is direct, practitioner, no hype. The core argument: enterprises are deploying agents without a policy layer and that's why they're failing — agents have no guardrails, make inconsistent decisions, and erode trust. The Niyam pattern fixes this by putting the business rules in Dataverse and making the agent policy-driven, not prompt-driven. Include specific failure examples from the field. Around 1,500 words."

### What the Agent Does

1. Opens with a hook that earns the click: "I've reviewed 23 enterprise AI pilots in the last 18 months. 17 of them failed. Not because the AI wasn't good enough. Because no one built the policy layer."

2. Structures the article in Ragnar's framework style:
   - Section 1: The pattern behind the failures (3 real failure archetypes, anonymized)
   - Section 2: Why "just prompt it better" doesn't scale (the fundamental problem with prompt-only agents)
   - Section 3: What the Niyam pattern is and why it works (Dataverse policy tables + D365 MCP + Power Automate, explained in 200 words)
   - Section 4: A concrete before/after example — AP approval agent with and without policy layer
   - Section 5: What this means for your next agent project (3 actionable takeaways)

3. Writes in Ragnar's voice throughout: first person, direct sentences, real numbers, no hedging, occasional blunt observation ("The uncomfortable truth is that most enterprise AI projects are prompt engineering experiments disguised as solutions.")

4. Adds the 3 LinkedIn formatting elements that drive engagement: the opening hook formatted as a standalone paragraph, bold sentence fragments at key points for mobile skimmability, and a closing question that invites comments

5. Appends the disclaimer: "Views expressed are my own and do not represent Microsoft's official position."

6. Flags 2 places where Ragnar should insert a specific client story if he has one to share — the article works without them but lands harder with real data

### The Business Impact

- **Article published in one session** instead of Ragnar spending a week drafting and redrafting
- **32,000 impressions in 7 days**, 847 reactions, 214 comments — best-performing article of the quarter
- **4 inbound consulting conversations** from CTOs who read the article and recognized their own failed pilots
- **Niyam pattern positioned** as a practitioner-defined framework, not a vendor solution

### Try It Yourself

> "I want to write a LinkedIn article about why Copilot Studio agents fail in production even when the demo looked great. The core argument is that agents built for demo conditions aren't built for the edge cases that appear in real business processes. Write the full article in Ragnar's voice, around 1,200 words."

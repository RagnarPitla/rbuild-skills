# Email Writer

## What This Skill Does

Takes any email situation and produces a draft ready to send or lightly edit. Works for the emails you've been putting off (difficult conversations, scope disputes, saying no) as well as the ones that just need to be done fast (follow-ups, meeting requests, updates). Different email types require different approaches, and this skill applies the right one automatically based on what you describe.

## Triggers

- "write an email"

## How It Works

### Step 1: Describe the Situation

Give these four things. The more specific, the better the draft.

```
1. Who you're writing to: [their name, role, and your relationship]
2. What you want them to do: [the one action you need]
3. Context: [any relevant history or details]
4. Tone: [formal / professional / casual / warm / assertive]
```

You don't need to write the email. Just explain it:

```
"Email to my client Tom. He's been adding work to our project without 
any budget conversation. Scope has grown by about 40% in 6 weeks. 
I need to address this professionally, propose two paths forward, and 
get a decision. Firm but not aggressive."
```

### Step 2: Understand the Right Approach by Type

**Cold outreach** (introducing yourself or pitching something):
Lead with what's relevant to them, not what you want. The first sentence should say something specific about them, their company, or their situation. Never open with "My name is" or "I represent."

**Follow-up** (after a proposal, meeting, or previous email):
Short and direct. Reference the specific thing you sent or discussed. Make the ask explicit. Don't apologize for following up.

**Difficult message** (scope dispute, delivering bad news, saying no):
Fact-based, not emotional. No blame language. State the situation clearly. Offer two paths forward, not one. End with a specific question or decision request.

**Apology** (you made a mistake, something went wrong):
Own it directly in the first sentence. Explain what you're doing differently. Keep it short. Do not over-explain or justify.

**Request** (asking for time, approval, an introduction):
State the ask in the first paragraph, not the last. Give the context that makes the ask reasonable. Make it easy to say yes with a specific option.

### Step 3: Apply the Universal Rules

**The one-ask rule:** One email, one ask. If you have two requests, send two emails. Multiple asks dramatically lower your response rate.

**Subject line formula:**
```
[Outcome they care about]: [Specific context]
"Proposal for Phase 2: [Client Name]"
"Quick question about Thursday's call"
"The contract: two paths forward"
```

**What to avoid:**
- "I hope this email finds you well" (skip it entirely)
- "Just following up" (say "Checking in on the proposal I sent Tuesday")
- Walls of text (4 sentences max per paragraph)
- Burying the ask in paragraph 3 (put it in paragraph 1)

## Output

A complete email draft with:
- Subject line
- Body (appropriately toned and structured for the type)
- Closing and signature placeholder

Optional: 2 subject line variations if the first is too formal or too casual.

## Checklist

- [ ] One ask, clearly stated in the first paragraph
- [ ] Subject line is specific, not generic
- [ ] No "I hope this email finds you well"
- [ ] Correct tone for the relationship and context
- [ ] Under 200 words for standard emails (requests, follow-ups, updates)
- [ ] Difficult messages use facts, not feelings
- [ ] Apology emails own the mistake in the first sentence

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Cause | Fix |
|-------|-------|-----|
| Too formal and stiff | No tone specified | Add "conversational" or "write like a real person, not a lawyer" |
| Too long | No length constraint | Add "under 100 words" or "3 sentences for the body" |
| Wrong angle | Incomplete context | Add the history: "We met at X, they previously said Y, the issue started when Z" |
| Difficult email too soft | Didn't specify assertiveness needed | Add "I need to be firm and direct. This has been a problem for 6 weeks." |

# Email Writer: Example

## Scenario: Freelance Developer Addresses a 40% Scope Creep and Gets a $4,500 Change Order Approved

**Who:** Tom Fischer, freelance software developer. Five years independent. Fixed-price project with a marketing agency client. The project was scoped at $11,000 for a custom client portal. Six weeks in, the scope has grown by at least 40% with no budget conversation.
**Where:** Email to the agency's account manager, who is friendly but non-committal about scope
**The Problem:** Tom has been delivering the extras without pushing back because he didn't want to damage the relationship. But he's now 80+ hours over his scoped estimate with no end in sight. He knows he needs to address it but has been avoiding the email for two weeks because he doesn't know how to raise it without sounding difficult. He's tried drafting it twice and both versions came out either too aggressive or too apologetic.

**Prerequisites:** None. Tom has 3 bullet points of context and a clear idea of what he needs.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Help me write a difficult email to a client. Here's the situation: I'm 6 weeks into a fixed-price project scoped at $11K. The scope has grown by about 40% through added features and changing requirements. I've been delivering without raising it but I can't keep going without a budget conversation. I need to address it professionally, propose two clear paths forward, and get a response. The relationship is good. I don't want to blow it up, but I need to be direct. Firm but professional."

### What the Agent Does

1. **Diagnoses the email type.** This is a scope dispute, which falls into the "difficult message" category. Claude identifies the structure: fact-based framing, no blame language, two paths forward, clear decision request.

2. **Drafts the subject line first.** Proposes two options: "Project Scope: Let's Align on the Path Forward" and "Client Portal Update: Scope and Next Steps." Tom picks the first one. It signals importance without alarm.

3. **Writes the opening with facts, not frustration.** Starts with: "As we approach the midpoint of the client portal build, I want to make sure we're aligned on scope and budget before we continue." No "I've been meaning to bring this up" and no apology for raising it.

4. **Quantifies the scope change clearly.** Includes a brief, factual list of the additions since the original scope document: user role system (added in week 2), CSV export feature (added in week 4), redesign of the dashboard layout (added in week 5). No editorial commentary. Just the list.

5. **Presents two paths forward** without demanding which one the client choose. Path A: adjust the contract to cover the actual scope, with a proposed change order of $4,500. Path B: finish the original scoped work only, with the additions treated as a Phase 2. Both options move the project forward.

6. **Closes with a decision request and a specific deadline.** "Could you let me know which direction you'd like to take by end of week? Happy to jump on a quick call if that's easier."

### The Business Impact

- **Response came within 2 hours.** The account manager replied: "This is fair, I should have flagged this earlier. Let's go with the change order."
- **$4,500 change order approved** the same afternoon. Tom invoiced it the next day.
- **Relationship intact.** The account manager thanked Tom for raising it professionally. They've since referred two additional clients.
- **Template reused.** Tom now has a scope conversation email template he sends at the first sign of scope creep, instead of waiting six weeks. Average time from sending to response: 4 hours.

### Try It Yourself

> "Now write the follow-up email for two weeks after the project wraps. I want to check in, make sure they're happy with the result, and mention that I have capacity for Phase 2 work. Keep it short and not salesy. The relationship is strong now."

# Article Writing — Example

## Scenario: Newsletter Article on Why Enterprise AI Projects Fail (and the Fix)

**Who:** Ragnar Pitla, AI Evangelist and founder of RBuild.ai, writing for his newsletter at ragnarsainewsletter.com
**Where:** Newsletter issue, 1,200 words, going out to an audience of enterprise practitioners, PMs, and architects
**The Problem:** Ragnar has a strong perspective — enterprise AI projects fail at a 70% rate not because of the technology, but because companies bolt AI onto broken processes. He also has the Niyam pattern as a concrete solution. He has 45 minutes before a back-to-back meeting block and needs a publication-ready draft, not a skeleton.

**Prerequisites:** None. The skill works from a topic brief, a core argument, and a target word count.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Write a 1,200-word newsletter article for ragnarsainewsletter.com. Topic: why 70% of enterprise AI projects fail. My argument: they fail because companies treat AI as a layer on top of bad processes, not as a forcing function to fix them. The fix is the Niyam pattern — policy-driven agents that encode the correct process in Dataverse and enforce it through the agent. Audience: enterprise PMs, architects, and IT leaders. My voice: confident practitioner, framework-driven, no buzzword bingo. No em dashes."

### What the Agent Does

Produces a complete, publication-ready article:

```
Title: The Real Reason Your AI Project Will Fail (And What to Do Instead)

The 70% failure statistic for enterprise AI projects gets cited at every
conference. What nobody explains is why.

It is not the model. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — they are all capable enough
to handle 90% of enterprise use cases. It is not the integration. Yes,
connecting to D365 or SAP is work, but it is solved work.

The real reason? You are putting AI on top of a broken process and calling
it a transformation.

Here is what that looks like in practice...

[SECTION: The Pattern I Keep Seeing]

A company comes to me with an AI agent project. They want to automate
purchase order approvals. The vision is compelling: an agent that reviews
POs, checks against policy, flags exceptions, and routes for approval
automatically.

We build it. It works in demo. Then three things happen...
```

The article includes four named sections, a framework callout box (the Niyam pattern explained in 200 words), and a CTA to the newsletter's skill library. Total word count lands at 1,190.

### The Business Impact

- **Draft to publish-ready in under 15 minutes** — versus 3 hours of staring at a blank document and rewriting the opening four times
- **Voice consistency** — the article reads like Ragnar wrote it, not like a generic AI article, because the skill encodes his practitioner framing
- **Reuse chain** — the same article becomes source material for a LinkedIn post, a YouTube script intro, and a podcast segment without rewriting from scratch

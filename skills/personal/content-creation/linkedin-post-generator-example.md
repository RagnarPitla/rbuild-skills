# LinkedIn Post Generator: Example

## Scenario: Supply Chain Consultant Turns a Client Win Into 3 Inbound Leads

**Who:** Marcus Webb, independent supply chain consultant. 12 years in operations, recently went solo. Strong expertise, uncomfortable with self-promotion. Posts on LinkedIn maybe once a month, usually gets 50-100 impressions.
**Where:** LinkedIn, professional audience of operations managers, logistics directors, and procurement leads.
**The Problem:** Marcus just finished a project where he saved a client $380K by fixing their demand forecasting formula. He wants to post about it but doesn't know how to make it interesting without sounding like he's bragging. He tries writing it himself and gets: "Excited to share that I recently helped a client save significant costs through improved demand planning." It's accurate. It's also invisible.

**Prerequisites:** None. Just the story and 2 minutes to answer a few questions.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Write a LinkedIn post. Here's the story: I just saved a client $380K by fixing their reorder point formula. The problem was they were using annual average demand instead of seasonal demand patterns. The fix took one afternoon once we identified the root cause. Audience: supply chain and operations professionals. I want thought leadership, not bragging. Under 250 words."

### What the Agent Does

1. **Identifies the real insight.** The dollar amount is not the story. The story is the gap between a complex-sounding problem and a simple fix, and what that says about how companies diagnose supply chain issues. The agent reframes the post around that insight.

2. **Writes three hook options** so Marcus can choose the angle that feels most like him:
   - "A client's $380K inventory problem had a one-afternoon fix. The hard part wasn't the math."
   - "Most demand forecasting problems aren't forecasting problems. They're data definition problems."
   - "We found a $380K mistake in a client's reorder formula in 20 minutes. It had been there for 3 years."

3. **Builds the post structure.** Marcus picks hook 3. The agent builds the tension (why does this keep happening, what everyone assumes the problem is) and the value (the actual diagnosis process, the specific mistake, what to check in your own system). Closes with a question: "What's the most expensive 'simple fix' you've found in an operation?"

4. **Keeps it tight.** 210 words. Three hashtags: #SupplyChain #OperationsExcellence #DemandPlanning. No "I'm thrilled to share."

### The Business Impact

- **12,400 impressions in 48 hours** (vs Marcus's usual 80-100)
- **3 inbound messages** from operations managers with similar inventory challenges, two of which turned into discovery calls
- **47 comments** including one from a VP of Supply Chain at a Fortune 500 company that sparked a separate connection
- **Pattern recognized:** Marcus now posts every Tuesday. Same formula. Specific number, real story, practical lesson, honest question at the end.

### Try It Yourself

> "Now write a follow-up post. The discovery call from that post led to a new project. I want to post about it without sounding like I'm just bragging about winning business. The real insight is that companies underestimate how much a single formula error can compound across 12 months of purchasing decisions. Audience is the same supply chain crowd. Keep it under 200 words."

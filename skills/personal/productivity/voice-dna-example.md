# Voice DNA — Example

## Scenario: The Marketing Head Whose AI Content Sounds Nothing Like Her

**Who:** Sarah Chen, Head of Marketing at a 120-person B2B software company, responsible for LinkedIn presence, newsletter, and thought leadership content.
**Where:** Claude.ai, used for all content drafting.
**The Problem:** Sarah has been using AI for 8 months to help draft content, but every piece it produces sounds polished and generic. Her real writing is punchy, data-first, and occasionally blunt. Her AI-written content sounds like a LinkedIn ghostwriter from 2019. Her team has started flagging the "off-brand" posts before they go out, which means she is still editing everything from scratch anyway.

**Prerequisites:** None. Just 3-5 pieces of writing that are genuinely yours.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Extract my Voice DNA from these samples. I'm going to paste 4 LinkedIn posts and 2 email threads I wrote to my team. Tell me: what are my sentence patterns, what words do I reach for, what do I never do, and what should AI never 'fix' about my writing?"

Sarah pastes 4 LinkedIn posts (ranging from 150 to 400 words each) and 2 internal team emails. She does not clean them up first.

### What the Agent Does

1. Reads all 6 samples and identifies patterns across them.
2. Calculates average sentence length (short, 8-12 words), paragraph length (2-3 lines max), and opening structure.
3. Identifies vocabulary: Sarah starts 60% of her professional posts with a data point or a statistic. She uses "real talk" as a phrase 3 times across the samples. She never uses passive voice. She ends 4 of 6 pieces with a direct question.
4. Flags override rules: she uses intentional single-word sentences as emphasis ("Period." "Done." "That's it."), she uses "but" to start sentences regularly, and she has a pattern of leading with the counterintuitive finding before explaining it.
5. Produces the Voice DNA card:

```
VOICE DNA — Sarah Chen

Sentence rhythm: Short and punchy. Average 9 words.
Intentional fragments used for emphasis. Fast reading pace.

Opening style: Leads with a surprising data point or
a contrarian take. Never with context-setting.
Example: "67% of marketers are doing this wrong."

Vocabulary profile: Plain, direct language. Prefers:
"build", "cut", "real", "here's what happened", "the data says".
Avoids: "leverage", "synergy", "thought leader", "delve", "robust".

Rhetorical habits: Ends with a direct question 80% of the time.
Uses "real talk" as a reset phrase. Starts sentences with "But".

Structural pattern: Lead with the punch. Context comes second.
Never buries the finding.

Override rules:
- Never "improve" single-word sentences used as emphasis
- Keep "But" at the start of sentences — intentional
- Short paragraphs always. 2-3 lines hard limit.
- Questions at the end are intentional, do not remove
- Never add "In conclusion" or any closing summary phrase
- The word "very" never appears
```

### The Business Impact

- **Content review time cut from 45 minutes to 5 minutes** — posts sound like Sarah on the first pass, not the third
- **Team stopped flagging off-brand content** — the override rules prevented the most common AI "improvements" that diluted her voice
- **Voice DNA now in Claude Projects** — Sarah pastes it into every project, so it applies without any extra prompting

### Try It Yourself

> "Now use my Voice DNA profile to write a LinkedIn post about the 3 marketing metrics most companies track that actually tell you nothing. Make it feel like I wrote it at 7am before I'd had coffee. Punchy. No fluff."

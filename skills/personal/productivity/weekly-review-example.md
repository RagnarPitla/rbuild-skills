# Weekly Review: Example

## Scenario: Product Manager Discovers She's Spending 40% of Her Week on the Wrong Work

**Who:** Priya Sharma, Senior Product Manager at a 150-person SaaS company. Manages two product lines, has a team of 4. Feels constantly busy but rarely satisfied. Has been meaning to ship two features for three months. Each Friday ends with a vague sense of "I was in meetings all week and I don't know what I actually got done."
**Where:** Claude, Friday afternoon, 10-minute session
**The Problem:** Priya is productive in the activity sense: she's in meetings, responding quickly, keeping stakeholders happy. But nothing is actually shipping. She has no system for closing out the week, so every Monday starts with roughly the same priority list as the previous Monday. After six weeks of this, two features that were "almost done" are still not done.

**Prerequisites:** None. Willingness to answer the five questions honestly.

---

### What You Say to the Agent

> "Weekly review time. Here's my week: I was in 22 meetings. Wrote a spec for the data export feature. Had three stakeholder calls. Reviewed a design mockup. Answered a lot of Slack. Meant to finish the pricing page requirements doc but didn't. Meant to clear my email backlog but didn't. Kept getting pulled into a support escalation that wasn't really mine to own."

### What the Agent Does

1. **Asks the five questions in sequence.** Claude starts with "What did you actually complete this week, specifically?" Priya realizes the answer is shorter than she expected: one spec written, one design reviewed, three calls completed. Everything else was reactive. The spec and design review are real completions.

2. **Digs into the "didn't happen" question.** Claude asks why the pricing page doc didn't happen. Priya says "I kept getting interrupted." Claude follows up: "Was that because you didn't block time, or because you accepted interruptions you could have declined?" Priya pauses. She accepted them because declining felt uncomfortable.

3. **Surfaces the pattern on "learned."** Priya says "I learned I'm bad at saying no." Claude reframes it as data: "You were pulled into a support escalation that wasn't yours to own. How many times has that happened in the last four weeks?" Priya realizes it happens almost every week. That's not a one-off.

4. **Forces a single priority.** Claude asks for the one most important thing for next week. Priya tries to say "finish both features." Claude pushes back: one thing. She chooses the pricing page requirements doc, because it's been blocking the engineering team for three weeks.

5. **Produces the summary.** Clean structured output. Accomplished: spec, design review, 3 stakeholder calls. Didn't happen: pricing doc, email backlog. Learned: reactive mode costs 40% of productive time. Next week's #1 priority: pricing page requirements doc, completed by Wednesday. Let go of: owning support escalations outside her product area.

### The Business Impact

- **Pattern emerged after 6 weeks of weekly reviews.** Priya tracked time across her review notes and found meetings consistently consumed 38-44% of her week. Only 12% of those meetings required her specifically.
- **Tuesday and Thursday mornings blocked.** She started declining non-essential meetings in those slots. No justification given, just "I have a standing commitment."
- **Two features shipped in the following 6 weeks** that had been stuck for three months. The pricing page doc was done by Wednesday of the first week she protected her mornings.
- **Team dynamic shifted.** Her team started seeing more finished work coming out of PM instead of more "updates on in-progress work."

### Try It Yourself

> "It's been 4 weeks of weekly reviews. Looking at my notes, I want to do a monthly reflection. What patterns should I look for across four weekly summaries, and how should I use them to adjust what I commit to in the next month?"
